AIStoryTeller: What a WIP Commit Cost Me

I pushed a WIP commit for sentence finishing, retry logic, and a world export snapshot on 2026-02-18. The next two days of repo history explain why I no longer treat that label lightly.

February 18, 20262 min read

Notes Build Logs Systems

WIP told the truth

I do not think every WIP commit is a mistake, but I do think every WIP commit tells the truth. On 2026-02-18 AIStoryTeller took in sentence-finish behavior, retry logic, and a world export snapshot in the same move. That is a useful bundle if I am just looking at capabilities. It is a dangerous bundle if I am thinking like an operator. Three state-sensitive ideas landed together: output repair, recovery behavior, and export visibility.

The export snapshot piece was smart. I like that part. When a story system starts tracking world state, having a readable snapshot is practical and honest. It gives me a way to inspect the system instead of only trusting it. But pairing that with output behavior changes and retry logic made the commit harder to judge. If something goes wrong after a move like that, I do not get one clean suspect. I get a cluster.

Momentum now, clarity later

The emotional part is familiar and not flattering. I know the sinking feeling of seeing a commit title tell me I moved faster than my verification habits. That feeling does not mean the work was bad. It means the sequencing was loose. In a project like this, where prose generation, status parsing, and UI state all touch each other, loose sequencing becomes cleanup work almost immediately.

What I learned

The concrete change was the addition of sentence-finish and retry behavior plus world export snapshotting. The lesson was sharper: recovery tooling should land separately from behavior changes whenever possible. If I need a snapshot to understand the system, that is already a sign I should reduce how many variables I change at once.

The repo history backs that lesson up. The very next day moved into repair work around NPC lists, raw toggles, and status fallbacks. That is the cost side of a fast WIP label. It buys momentum now and spends clarity later.

Next move: split recovery tools, UI parsing fixes, and generation behavior into cleaner, testable slices.
