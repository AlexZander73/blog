FitnessCoach: Turning a Flat App into a Navigation System

I moved from screens that merely existed to flows that actually connected. That shift changed the product from a static dashboard feel into something I could navigate with intent.

March 22, 20264 min read

Design Apps Build Logs

The app could do things, but not yet flow

By 2026-03-17, I had enough base functionality to see a new problem: the app could do things, but the path between those things was too flat. I added docs/sprint-roadmap.md and then shipped a broad UX layer in 58910d6 with detail routes, session surfaces, and analytics drill-down. I felt both excited and slightly overwhelmed because this was the first time the product started behaving like a real workflow instead of a demo.

Adding depth and drill-down

The concrete change was structural navigation depth. I added route-level pages for workout detail, exercise detail, plan-day detail, session recap, and progress metric detail. I also made previously static cards and list rows open meaningful contexts, so users could move from summary to action without dead ends.

This mattered because habit products live or die on friction. If I cannot go from "today overview" to "exact movement cue" in one or two taps, I probably will not use the app consistently. The sprint plan made that problem explicit and gave me an implementation sequence that was practical instead of chaotic.

Thinking in loops instead of screens

The lesson for me was simple: feature quantity does not equal usability. Navigation intent has to be designed as a product surface. I stopped thinking in isolated screens and started thinking in user loops: check in, adjust, train, recap, review trend, repeat.

That shift also made future prioritization easier. Once the loops are visible, missing pieces stand out quickly. I can now evaluate new work by asking whether it shortens or clarifies one of those loops.

Next move: add stronger iPad-specific split-pane behavior to make planner and analytics exploration faster on larger screens.
