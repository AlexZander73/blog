FitnessCoach: Building a Deterministic Engine I Can Defend

I committed to deterministic recommendations because I wanted every adjustment to be explainable and reviewable. That forced more structure, but it also made the safety story honest.

March 19, 20264 min read

Systems Notes Apps

Why determinism mattered here

One of my non-negotiables in FitnessCoach is that recommendation output should be deterministic, not improvisational. I wanted to be able to point to a rule and say, "this is why the workout changed." When I worked through packages/rules-engine and the associated docs, I felt a quiet kind of confidence I rarely get with fuzzy systems. The logic may be strict, but it is legible.

Building a pipeline I could explain

The concrete change was the pipeline itself: goal selection, candidate scoring, hard-constraint filtering, safety screen, substitutions, low-time and low-energy modifiers, then final workout composition with explanation lines. That structure is documented and tested. It also includes conservative escalation behavior instead of vague warnings, which matters a lot in a recovery-aware app.

The hard part is that determinism moves effort from inference to curation. I had to carry explicit substitution rules, contraindication tags, and structured symptom fields. There is no shortcut where a model invents plausible advice. If content quality is weak, output quality is weak. That is both limitation and benefit.

The engineering constraint hiding inside the product

I learned that explainability is not just a nice product trait. It is an engineering constraint that affects data models, API contracts, and UX copy. Once I accepted that, the rest of the architecture made more sense. The rules engine became the center, not an add-on.

This choice also changed how I write tests. I do not test "smartness." I test expected behavior under defined conditions. That is less flashy, but it gives me a stable foundation to iterate from as the project grows.

Next move: expand safety test coverage from core red flags into more body-area-specific substitution edge cases.
