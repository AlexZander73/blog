Social Post Helper: Why I Chose a Broad Safari File Filter

I ended up with a broader upload filter than I originally wanted. It was the right compromise because browser compatibility mattered more than a perfectly tidy picker.

March 14, 20262 min read

Apps Systems Build Logs

From no filter to a better compromise

After removing the strict upload filter to unblock the macOS Photos picker, I still wanted some structure back. A completely unrestricted picker was useful as a diagnostic step, but it was not where I wanted the product to stay. The committed change on 2026-03-14 was the compromise: use a broad image/*,video/* accept filter instead of the long, specific list I had before. I remember feeling a little uneasy about that. Broad filters look less disciplined. In this case, though, they were more honest.

Moving strictness deeper into the app

The concrete change was small in the diff and large in effect. Rather than trying to enumerate every extension and MIME combination the picker might interpret badly, I moved the precision deeper into the app. The picker got broader. The validation logic stayed responsible for deciding what the MVP actually supports. That split turned out to fit the problem much better.

What I learned here is that strictness in the wrong layer can be a bug. I had been trying to make the file dialog do policy enforcement. That sounds clean until it collides with Safari, macOS Photos integration, and format labeling differences. Once that happened, the better architecture was obvious: let the OS help the user get to their media, then let the app judge what it can handle.

Forgiving UI, strict validation

There is still a tradeoff. A broader filter means users may select files that the app later rejects. That is not ideal. But a rejected file with a clear message is better than a greyed-out picker that feels broken and unexplained. One is a manageable product edge. The other is a trust problem.

This change sharpened my thinking about front-end validation. User-facing flexibility and internal strictness are not opposites. They often need each other.

Next move: keep unsupported-file feedback specific so the broader picker feels forgiving, not sloppy.
