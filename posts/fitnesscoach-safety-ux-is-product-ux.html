FitnessCoach: Safety UX Is Product UX

Safety messaging is not a legal footer in this project; it is part of the core interaction model. I added red-flag handling and conservative pathways so the app can adapt without pretending to diagnose.

March 24, 20264 min read

Systems Design Notes

Treating safety as core interaction work

I treat safety behavior as core product work, not compliance garnish. In this repository, that shows up in the rule-engine safety pipeline, red-flag check-in flow, and explicit conservative language. I felt the emotional weight of this area more than any other part, because careless wording in fitness and pain contexts can do real harm.

Building conservative pathways on purpose

The concrete change included updates to safety logic plus dedicated safety tests, and UI support for red-flag routing in the client. The system now models caution and stop-and-refer states, and avoids diagnostic claims by design. That made the language harder to write, but far more responsible.

The tradeoff is that conservative behavior can feel restrictive. There are moments where a user might want a harder recommendation and the system intentionally refuses. I accepted that constraint because trust matters more than short-term engagement spikes, especially in symptom-aware flows.

If I cannot explain it, I should not ship it

The lesson here was direct: if I cannot explain a safety decision clearly, I should not ship it. Deterministic outputs made this possible because every adjustment has a traceable rule path, not a black-box answer.

I also realized how much safety quality depends on content governance. Rules alone are not enough. Contraindication tags, substitution mappings, and educational copy all need review discipline to stay safe over time. That is why the admin and content workflow matter more than they seem.

Next move: expand clinician-reviewed tagging and formalize a review checklist for high-risk symptom pathways before release.
